OVERVIEW

Citation:          Mazur, A. and Alcaide, E. (2026). On Expressiveness and Mechanism Design of Linear Recurrent Models. Unpublished paper.
Abstract Summary:  This thesis investigates the transferability of mechanism-level extensions across linear-time recurrent sequence models, specifically RWKV, Mamba-$2$, and Linear Attention. It proposes three mechanism-level extensions—Multi-Scale Depthwise Convolution, Damped Harmonic Oscillator, and Chunked Value Decorrelation—and evaluates their impact on expressivity deficits across different causal backbones.
Key Methodology:   The study employs a cross-architecture transfer matrix, populating it with three causal backbones, two access modes (causal and LION bidirectional), two parameter scales ($7$M and $14$M), and three probes (LibriSpeech, Common Voice English, Multi-Query Associative Recall).
Research Question: Can the expressivity of causal linear-time recurrent architectures be characterized through an axis decomposition under which a mechanism's gain on a task is predictable from the residual structural deficit the architecture leaves uncovered along the axis the mechanism targets?

EDITOR'S NOTE

Reviewer 2 raises valid methodological concerns, but many of these stem from treating a resource-constrained thesis as a fully resourced institutional paper. The primary defense against the demand for multi-seed validation and generic structural controls is the explicit compute limitation already stated (p. 35). Mazur and Alcaide could argue that the matrix's value lies in its macro-level directional coherence across sixty-five cells, which provides aggregate statistical confidence that offsets individual cell variance. To address the mixed-precision confound, the authors could clarify that bf16 was a hardware necessity for the Linear Attention LION-mode cells, but argue that since lower precision typically degrades or maintains performance rather than artificially inflating it, the emergence of the predicted structural pattern under these constraints makes the finding more robust, not less.

To satisfy the reviewer's semantic critiques without compromising the core argument, several terminology adjustments could be helpful. The term "proportional" could be replaced with "monotonic" or "rank-ordered" throughout the paper, as the authors already clarify that the claim relies on absolute delta ordering rather than strict mathematical proportionality (p. 38). Similarly, the authors might replace the word "orthogonal" with "conceptually distinct" when describing the expressivity axes, acknowledging the reviewer's astute observation that the Multi-Scale Depthwise Convolution mechanism functionally couples the axes by bootstrapping the associative recall task. Furthermore, all instances of "pre-registered" could be changed to "pre-specified" or "hypothesized." This avoids the formal scientific implication of a time-stamped repository while preserving the timeline of intent.

The authors should also consider correcting the clerical sign error where the composition delta for RWKV-6 is incorrectly listed as a positive $0.0003$ instead of the negative $0.0003$ supported by the data (pp. 44, 56). Regarding the undocumented noise floor, a brief methodological sentence could be added explaining exactly which prior matched-budget runs generated the $0.0014$ standard deviation estimate (p. 35). For the missing null-effect data relegated to the discovery-phase archive, the authors might explicitly reframe the alignment criterion as a predictive heuristic supported primarily by its positive-transfer half, formally acknowledging the absence of the null-matrix as a limitation for future work rather than a hidden flaw.

If defending the alignment criterion as a formal framework proves too difficult against the reviewer's demands for rigorous null-testing and generic controls, a fallback strategy could be to dial back the ambition of the theoretical claim. In this scenario, the authors could remove the formal mechanism-prior alignment criterion entirely and reframe the paper as an exploratory empirical characterization of linear recurrent models. This involves jettisoning the theoretical rigidity of the axis decomposition and presenting the transfer matrix purely as an observational study of architectural patches, which neutralizes the reviewer's attacks on orthogonality and proportionality while preserving the publishable empirical results.

SUMMARY
========================================================================

Is It Credible?
The paper investigates the expressiveness of linear-time recurrent sequence models, specifically focusing on the RWKV-6, Mamba-2, and Linear Attention architectures. The central claim is the "mechanism-prior alignment criterion," which posits that a mechanism's performance gain on a given task is proportional to the residual structural deficit the architecture leaves uncovered along the axis the mechanism targets, and is null when the task does not exercise that axis. To support this, Mazur and Alcaide introduce three mechanism-level extensions---Multi-Scale Depthwise Convolution (MSDC), Damped Harmonic Oscillator (DHO), and Chunked Value Decorrelation (CVD)---and evaluate them across a 65-cell cross-architecture transfer matrix. A major empirical takeaway is that linear-time backbones augmented with MSDC or CVD can successfully solve the Multi-Query Associative Recall (MQAR) task at a sequence length of 1024, a regime where the causal Transformer baseline fails. 

While the mechanism-prior alignment criterion offers an elegant conceptual heuristic, the empirical matrix used to validate it suffers from several methodological limitations that weaken the certainty of the quantitative claims. Mazur and Alcaide explicitly acknowledge that the matrix relies on single-seed training runs for every reported cell due to compute constraints, meaning per-cell standard errors are not estimated (p. 49). To address this, the paper relies on the directional coherence of the across-matrix pattern to provide aggregate statistical confidence. However, the paper defines a "BREAK" band for performance changes based on an estimated noise floor that is derived from prior matched-budget runs (p. 35). Because the data and methods used to calculate this noise floor are not documented, relying on it to establish significance thresholds for single-seed runs leaves the precise categorization of the results uncertain. Furthermore, the paper repeatedly describes the relationship between mechanism gain and architectural deficit as "proportional" (p. 10). The data actually demonstrates a monotonic rank-order relationship rather than strict mathematical proportionality; for instance, the absolute gains for MSDC at the 7M causal scale show a much larger gap between Linear Attention and RWKV-6 than between RWKV-6 and Mamba-2 (pp. 37--38). 

The testing of the alignment criterion is also incomplete. The hypothesis is two-sided, requiring that mechanisms provide no gain when their target axis is not exercised. Mazur and Alcaide acknowledge that the matrix systematically omits evidence for this null-effect side, leaving it to an unseen "discovery-phase archive" (p. 47). By relying on unpublished data for half of the theoretical criterion, the claim that mechanisms only work in a targeted way remains partially unsupported. Additionally, the paper introduces a hardware-driven confound in its bidirectional falsification test. Mazur and Alcaide note that the Linear Attention LION-mode cells were trained in bf16 precision rather than the fp32 baseline due to memory pressure (p. 51). Because lower precision can alter optimization dynamics, this procedural inconsistency makes it difficult to definitively isolate the architectural effects of the decay mechanism from the training dynamics introduced by the precision switch.

Finally, the theoretical framing of the axes of expressivity as "orthogonal" is complicated by the empirical results (p. 10). The paper shows that MSDC, designed to target short-range temporal structure, successfully bootstraps the MQAR task, which is the designated probe for associative memory under interference (pp. 43--44). Mazur and Alcaide explain that MSDC helps by producing consistent local representations early in training, which demonstrates a functional coupling between the axes rather than strict orthogonality. Similarly, the paper does not fully rule out the possibility that the mechanisms improve the general optimization landscape rather than supplying specific structural inductive biases. Without control experiments comparing the mechanisms to generic parameter-matched additions like simple residual blocks, the exact source of the performance gains remains somewhat ambiguous. Despite these constraints, the paper provides a highly useful directional framework for architecture design and compelling evidence that augmented linear-time models can overcome specific associative recall bottlenecks.

The Bottom Line

The paper presents an intriguing theoretical framework for understanding expressivity deficits in linear-time recurrent models and demonstrates that targeted mechanisms can solve specific recall tasks where standard baselines fail. However, the central mechanism-prior alignment criterion is only partially supported due to single-seed experimental designs, untested null hypotheses, and unablated optimization confounds. While the strict proportionality and orthogonality claims are overstated, the work offers a valuable and structurally rigorous heuristic for evaluating and patching linear-time architectures.

POTENTIAL ISSUES
========================================================================

Statistical significance and noise floor estimation: The empirical foundation of the paper relies on single-seed training runs for its 65-cell cross-architecture transfer matrix. Mazur and Alcaide explicitly acknowledge this limitation, stating that the matrix is single-seed for every reported cell and that per-cell standard errors are not estimated, justifying this as a deliberate scope decision under the available compute (p. 49). To compensate, the paper relies on the directional coherence of the across-matrix pattern to provide aggregate statistical confidence (p. 35). However, this acknowledgement does not fully resolve the analytical weakness of making fine-grained quantitative claims without variance estimates. Furthermore, the paper defines a "BREAK" band for performance changes based on an estimated noise floor of $\sigma \approx 0.0014$, which is derived from prior matched-budget runs at the codebase configuration (p. 35). The data, methods, and specific architectures used to calculate this noise floor are not documented in the paper or appendices. Relying on an unverifiable noise floor to establish significance thresholds for single-seed runs leaves the precise quantitative categorization of the results uncertain.

Mixed-precision training confound: The methodology introduces a potential confound by varying the numerical precision across models. Table A.1 specifies the numerical precision as the fp32 baseline, and bf16 only for the LA LION-mode cells where memory pressure required it (p. 51). Mazur and Alcaide acknowledge this hardware-driven necessity. However, training with lower precision (bf16) can alter model convergence, stability, and final performance compared to fp32. The paper does not provide a control experiment or sensitivity analysis to demonstrate that this precision switch did not impact the results. This is particularly concerning because the Linear Attention LION-mode cells are central to the critical finding that decay is the prerequisite for the value-decorrelation mechanism in the bidirectional setting (p. 39). The procedural inconsistency compromises the matched-budget design, making it difficult to definitively disentangle the architectural effects of the decay mechanism from the training dynamics introduced by bf16 precision.

Incomplete testing of the alignment criterion: The mechanism-prior alignment criterion is defined as a two-sided hypothesis: a mechanism should provide a gain when its target axis is exercised by the task, and it should be null when the mechanism's axis is not exercised by the task (p. 10). However, the paper systematically omits evidence for the second, null effect side. Mazur and Alcaide explicitly acknowledge this gap, stating that the evidence for the converse half, where a mechanism engages under stochastic gradient descent without converting into measured task gain, is not exercised by the matrix and is left to the discovery-phase archive (p. 47). While the failure of the DHO mechanism on the MQAR task is presented as one example of an axis mismatch (p. 44), the broader pattern of null results for other mechanisms on other tasks is absent from the document. By relying on an unseen archive for half of the theoretical criterion, the claim that mechanisms only work in a targeted way remains partially unsupported by the presented matrix evidence.

Overstatement of proportionality: The paper repeatedly uses the word "proportional" to describe the relationship between a mechanism's gain and the architectural deficit it addresses (pp. 10, 22, 47). In mathematical terms, proportionality implies a strict linear relationship. Because the deficit is a qualitative construct rather than a quantified metric, this mathematical relationship cannot be formally tested. The empirical data supports a monotonic rank-order relationship rather than strict proportionality. For example, the absolute gains for MSDC at the 7M causal scale are Linear Attention ($-0.0470$), RWKV-6 ($-0.0261$), and Mamba-2 ($-0.0211$) (pp. 37--38). The gap between Linear Attention and RWKV-6 ($0.0209$) is substantially larger than the gap between RWKV-6 and Mamba-2 ($0.0050$). Mazur and Alcaide clarify that the deficit-proportional ordering claim is argued in absolute $\Delta$ terms (p. 38), but the repeated use of the mathematically precise term "proportional" overstates the ordinal relationship demonstrated by the data.

Optimization landscape confound: The paper attributes performance gains to the specific architectural function of each mechanism, such as local mixing for MSDC or phase representation for DHO. However, the experimental design does not fully rule out the alternative explanation that these mechanisms simply improve the model's general optimization landscape. For example, MSDC introduces parallel convolutional paths that are summed, which is structurally similar to a residual network and could provide shorter, more effective gradient paths independent of capturing temporal hierarchy. Similarly, DHO introduces new parameterizations that might act as a form of regularization. The paper does not include control experiments---such as comparing MSDC to a simple residual block with equivalent parameters---to definitively disentangle the claimed architectural inductive biases from potential confounding optimization benefits.

Orthogonality and conceptual slippage: The paper frames its conceptual contribution around an axis decomposition of expressivity, describing these axes as orthogonal (p. 10). In mathematics, orthogonality implies strict independence. However, the empirical results show that the Multi-Scale Depthwise Convolution (MSDC), designed to target Axis 1 (short-range temporal structure), successfully bootstraps the Multi-Query Associative Recall (MQAR) task, which is the designated probe for Axis 2 (associative memory under interference) (pp. 43--44). The explanation that MSDC helps by producing consistent local representations early in training demonstrates a functional coupling between the axes, suggesting they are distinct but not strictly orthogonal (p. 44). Furthermore, there is a conceptual slippage between the formal definition of expressivity as sequence-transformation capacity and its operationalization through applied benchmarks like Character Error Rate (p. 10). While Mazur and Alcaide explicitly define this methodology, treating a noisy empirical metric as a direct proxy for formal structural capacity introduces an epistemological tension into the framework.

Mechanism versus architecture-specific patch: The paper distinguishes its proposed transferable structural primitives from architecture-specific patches (p. 1). However, the implementation details reveal that the mechanisms require significant architecture-specific tailoring. For instance, the Chunked Value Decorrelation (CVD) mechanism is applied differently across backbones: for RWKV-6, the key-similarity source is the keys produced by the Time-Mix projection; for Mamba-2, it is the chunk-local $C_c$ tensor; and for Linear Attention, it is the feature-mapped key sequence $\phi(K_c)$ (p. 30). Mazur and Alcaide acknowledge that CVD has three backbone-specific forms because the chunk structure of the substrate differs across backbones (p. 30). While the logical operation is shared, the necessity of deeply integrating the mechanism into the specific tensor routing of each architecture stretches the definition of a universally transferable primitive, making it function more as a generalizable design pattern for creating tailored patches.

Unjustified compositions and internal ablations: The empirical evaluation lacks ablation studies to justify several internal design choices and composition selections. First, the paper evaluates mechanism composition by testing only one specific pair of mechanisms for each backbone (CVD $\times$ MSDC on RWKV-6 and Mamba-2; DHO $\times$ MSDC on Linear Attention) (p. 36). The paper does provide a structural rationale for these composition choices, stating that composition stacks where the two mechanisms target distinct deficits on the architecture's operator chain, and saturates where the second mechanism intersects the deficit the first already addresses (pp. 44--46). However, the lack of exhaustive pairwise testing (or testing alternative pairings like DHO $\times$ CVD) leaves the conclusion vulnerable to concerns of selective reporting. Second, the proposed mechanisms contain fixed internal hyperparameters without empirical ablations. For example, the DHO mechanism uses a specific depth-graded $\theta$-clip schedule with values like $\pi/8$ and $\pi/4$ (p. 26), and MSDC uses a fixed dilation set of $\{1, 2, 4, 8\}$ (p. 24). While qualitative structural motivations and literature precedents are provided, the absence of ablations makes it unclear how sensitive the mechanisms' performance is to these specific, fixed choices.

Terminology regarding pre-registration: The paper repeatedly claims to have pre-registered hypotheses, probes, and experimental choices, such as the pre-registered probe property for Common Voice English (p. 42) and pre-registered composition cells (p. 44). In formal scientific practice, pre-registration requires archiving a research plan in a public, time-stamped repository prior to conducting the study to prevent post-hoc hypothesizing. The document provides no evidence of formal registration, such as a DOI or a link to an external repository. While the authors use the term to signal that their hypotheses were defined prior to running the experimental matrix, using this specific terminology without a formal registry does not align with the established definition of pre-registration, which could lead to an inaccurate perception of the research's procedural rigor and makes the claims of prior intent unverifiable.

Clerical error in composition gain: There is a contradiction between the narrative text and the reported data regarding the performance of the CVD $\times$ MSDC composition on the RWKV-6 7M model. The paper claims that the composition saturates on RWKV-6, stating the $\Delta$ over MSDC alone is $+0.0003$ at 7M (p. 44), which implies that adding CVD harmed performance. However, Table B.1 reports the RWKV-6 MSDC Test CER as $0.0788$ and the RWKV-6 CVD $\times$ MSDC Test CER as $0.0785$ (p. 56). The calculation ($0.0785 - 0.0788$) results in a change of $-0.0003$, which is an improvement. The narrative text contains a sign error when referencing the data in the table.

========================================================================
FUTURE RESEARCH
========================================================================

Multi-seed validation and variance estimation: Future work should execute multi-seed training runs for the core cells of the cross-architecture transfer matrix. This would allow for the calculation of formal standard errors and the establishment of a rigorous, verifiable noise floor, replacing the undocumented threshold used to categorize performance gains.

Isolating optimization confounds: To definitively prove that the performance gains stem from specific architectural inductive biases rather than general optimization improvements, future studies should include generic structural controls. For example, comparing the Multi-Scale Depthwise Convolution to a generic residual block with an equivalent parameter count would help isolate the specific benefit of the temporal hierarchy prior.

Rigorous testing of the null hypothesis: The two-sided mechanism-prior alignment criterion requires proof that mechanisms do not provide gains when their target axis is unexercised. Future research should formalize and publish the data from the discovery-phase archive to empirically validate this null-effect side of the hypothesis across the full matrix.

Standardized precision controls: To eliminate the mixed-precision confound in the bidirectional falsification tests, future experiments should re-run the Linear Attention LION-mode cells in fp32 precision. If memory pressure remains an issue, techniques such as gradient checkpointing or reduced batch sizes could be employed to ensure the numerical precision remains consistent across all compared architectures.

COPYEDITING

The manuscript presents a compelling and structurally rigorous framework for evaluating linear-time recurrent models. However, there are several areas where the terminology overstates the mathematical precision of the findings, and a few methodological caveats require clearer documentation. The general pattern of issues revolves around the use of strict formal terms (such as proportional, orthogonal, and pre-registered) for relationships that are ordinal, conceptually distinct, or hypothesized. Additionally, addressing unstated assumptions regarding the noise floor, mixed-precision training, and null-effect testing will significantly strengthen the manuscript's credibility.

- **p. 10** "The expressivity of causal linear-time recurrent architectures does not collapse to a single scalar; it decomposes into orthogonal axes" The word orthogonal implies strict mathematical independence, but the empirical results demonstrate functional coupling, such as a mechanism targeting short-range structure bootstrapping an associative memory task. Consider replacing orthogonal with conceptually distinct here and throughout the theoretical framing to avoid overstating the independence of these axes.

- **p. 11** "A mechanism's gain on a given task is proportional to the residual structural deficit the architecture leaves uncovered" The term proportional implies a strict mathematical linear relationship, which cannot be formally tested since the deficit is a qualitative construct. The empirical data instead supports an ordinal relationship. Consider revising proportional to monotonic or rank-ordered here and throughout the manuscript (e.g., on pp. ii, 1, 22, 38, 47) to accurately reflect the empirical findings.

- **p. 23** "We pre-register Common Voice English [3]" The term pre-register formally implies the existence of a time-stamped research plan in a public repository, which is not provided in the text. To avoid implying formal registration while preserving the timeline of intent, consider changing all instances of pre-registered to pre-specified or hypothesized throughout the document.

- **p. 35** "The empirical noise floor on test character error rate between matched re-runs at the codebase configuration is approximately $\sigma \approx 0.0014$, estimated from prior matched-budget runs." Relying on an undocumented noise floor to establish significance thresholds leaves the quantitative categorization uncertain. Consider inserting a brief sentence detailing exactly which prior matched-budget runs and architectures were used to calculate this $0.0014$ standard deviation estimate.

- **p. 47** "The converse half, where a mechanism engages under stochastic gradient descent without converting into measured task gain, is not exercised by the matrix and is left to the discovery-phase archive that preceded the 50-epoch budget." Relying on unpublished data for half of the theoretical criterion leaves the null-effect side of the hypothesis partially unsupported. Consider softening this language to explicitly frame the lack of null-effect testing as a limitation of the current study for future work, rather than treating the unseen archive as an established proof.

- **p. 51** "Numerical precision fp32 baseline; bf16 only for the LA LION-mode cells where memory pressure required it" Using lower precision introduces a potential confound that could alter training dynamics, making it difficult to isolate the architectural effects of the decay mechanism. Consider adding a short caveat noting that while the use of bf16 introduces variance in training dynamics due to hardware necessities, it typically maintains or degrades performance rather than artificially inflating it, meaning it does not invalidate the directional findings.
